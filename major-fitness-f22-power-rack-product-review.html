Executive Summary

Buy the Major Fitness F22 if you want one garage-gym centerpiece for barbell lifts, cable work, pull-ups, dips, rows, and landmine exercises—and you have a permanent space roughly five feet by five-and-a-half feet with enough ceiling clearance. Skip it if smooth, fast selectorized cable work is your top priority, or if you need equipment that folds away after every session.

The pitch is simple: replace several machines with one rack.

The tradeoff is equally simple. The F22 is a capable plate-loaded generalist, not a commercial functional trainer. Its value comes from versatility and included attachments, while its compromises show up in assembly, plate changes, cable smoothness, hole spacing, and the amount of planning required before a 278-pound rack arrives.

Skip If

The F22 can become an expensive obstacle if the room, training style, or ownership expectations are wrong.

  • You need the rack to fold against a wall or share a parking space daily.
  • You mainly train with cables and expect the glide of a dedicated selectorized functional trainer.
  • Your ceiling is close to the rack's 82.5-inch height or too low for comfortable pull-ups.
  • You dislike loading plates onto two pulley sides or changing setups between exercises.
  • You want commercial-grade attachment compatibility with tighter one- or two-inch hole spacing.

Price Reality Check

The standard F22 is typically positioned around $930 before package upgrades. That places it above entry-level Amazon cages but below premium rack-plus-selectorized-cable systems from REP, Force USA, and similar brands.

At this price, compare the complete gym—not just the rack.

The base machine still requires a barbell, bench, and plates for most strength programs. Bundles can push the total toward $1,400 to $2,300 depending on included weights. Convenience is real, but experienced buyers may save money—or improve quality—by sourcing the bench, bar, and plates separately.

Deep Dive

Four Training Systems in One Footprint

The F22 combines a power rack, dual adjustable pulley system, multi-grip pull-up bar, and 360-degree landmine. In buyer terms, that means heavy compound lifts and lighter accessory work can happen without walking between separate machines.

The buyer benefit is exercise coverage, not luxury feel.

  • Barbell squats, presses, rack pulls, and bench work use the included J-hooks and safety arms.
  • Two independent pulleys support unilateral moves, flyes, rows, curls, and pressdowns.
  • The low-row footplate, landmine, pull-up bar, dip handles, and included cable handles broaden the program immediately.
  • The 2:1 pulley ratio makes cable movement longer and lighter than the plates loaded onto each side.

Buyer takeaway: This is strongest as a first all-purpose gym. A specialist who already owns a premium rack may prefer a dedicated cable machine instead.

Capacity Claims Versus Everyday Training

Major Fitness lists substantial capacities for the J-hooks, safety arms, pull-up bar, and cable system. Those numbers are reassuring, but most buyers will notice stability, spacing, and ergonomics long before reaching the maximum load.

  • The rack weighs about 277.8 pounds and uses a broad triangular base for stability.
  • Its 64.2-by-58.2-inch footprint is compact compared with multiple machines, but large compared with a basic squat stand.
  • The standard rack uses relatively wide spacing between upright and pulley adjustment positions.
  • Independent testing found it stable under meaningful loads, while noting that nothing about the finish feels truly premium.

Buyer takeaway: Buy for the exercises you can perform safely—not because a four-digit capacity number looks impressive.

Cable System: Useful, but Not Selectorized

The dual pulley system is the feature that separates the F22 from a normal power cage. It is also where expectations need the most calibration.

The plate-loaded design keeps cost down, but changing resistance requires moving plates. One hands-on reviewer also found the stock cables and pulleys less smooth than dedicated selectorized trainers from higher-end brands.

Buyer takeaway: Occasional cable accessories fit the F22 well. Cable-dominant bodybuilding workouts may justify spending more on a weight-stack system.

Real-World Ownership Check

A rack this size is closer to installing furniture than unboxing fitness gear. Delivery, assembly, ceiling height, floor protection, plate storage, and exercise clearance are part of the purchase.

  • Setup: Expect multiple boxes, many bolts, at least two capable adults, and several hours of organized assembly.
  • Space: Measure the rack, pull-up headroom, barbell width, bench travel, cable paths, and plate-loading access—not only the listed footprint.
  • Maintenance: Periodically check bolts, cables, pulleys, carabiners, and wear surfaces. Keep the cable path clean and correctly tensioned.
  • Workflow: Plate-loaded cables add setup time when moving between heavy and light exercises.
  • Deal breaker: If the rack blocks a vehicle, door, HVAC unit, or storage area, its versatility will not make the room frustration disappear.

What I Would Compare Next

Compare by space, cable workflow, and upgrade path—not by the number of exercises printed in the marketing.

  • Major Fitness Drone models: Compare if Amazon pricing is substantially lower and you can accept lighter-duty compromises.
  • Major Fitness F35: Compare if reclaiming floor space matters more than the deeper four-post structure.
  • Major Fitness F22 Pro: Compare if selectorized stacks, tighter adjustments, and heavier construction justify roughly double the price.
  • Force USA G3: Compare if a Smith machine and a more established premium all-in-one platform matter.
  • REP rack plus functional-trainer system: Compare if long-term modularity, attachment compatibility, and cable refinement matter more than initial cost.
